Year 2 – Technical Game Producer Track

In the second year of the self-study MFA-equivalent program, the focus shifts to multiplayer games and advanced production skills. This page summarizes the Fall and Spring courses, plus a semester-long Capstone Project that culminates in a playable, released multiplayer game.

Each row below outlines the course’s name, key topics, approximate credits, major activities, and final outcome. Expand the “Detailed Curriculum” section to read about each course’s deeper goals, resources, and relevance for the Technical Game Producer role.

At the bottom, you’ll find the suggested Part-Time Self-Study Schedule (Weeks 1–30) that blends all course content with the Capstone milestones.

Detailed Curriculum (Click to Expand)

This course deepens design expertise by focusing on complex game systems, level design, and narrative design. Students practice advanced iteration and systems thinking. Key assignments include building tabletop prototypes to explore economies and balancing, analyzing environmental storytelling in professional games, and writing short narrative design docs.

Relevance: For a Technical Game Producer, it’s crucial to understand design decisions and communicate effectively with designers. Mastery of systems design helps guide scoping decisions, ensuring technology and design remain in sync.

Resources & Examples: “Rules of Play” (Salen & Zimmerman), advanced GDC talks on systemic design & emergent narrative, and Case Studies from AAA or indie projects that highlight design iteration.

  • Lag compensation, matchmaking, client-server sync
  • Rapid local multiplayer prototypes
  • Implementing a client-server demo
  • Midterm: small-scale online game

 Experience designing & coding networked gameplay; one complete online prototype for portfolio.
Detailed Curriculum (Click to Expand)

Dedicated to network programming and multiplayer design, this course covers both local co-op/competitive design fundamentals and full online netcode. Students learn client-server architecture, authoritative servers, client prediction, matchmaking, and more. Half the course emphasizes design (local multiplayer prototypes), and the second half focuses on coding a functional networked game in Unity or Unreal.

Relevance: A Technical Game Producer must grasp the complexities of netcode to schedule tasks, plan infrastructure, and anticipate typical pitfalls (lag, scaling, cheat prevention). Hands-on coding clarifies networking’s real requirements and trade-offs.

Resources & Examples: Official Unity Netcode docs, Gabriel Gambetta’s “Fast-Paced Multiplayer” articles, GDC talks on Overwatch and Halo netcode.

  • Version control, build pipelines
  • Leadership & conflict resolution
  • Simulate scrum sprints, user stories
  • Practice daily standups & retrospectives
  • Set up CI & version control for group prototypes
  • Team leadership roleplay & case studies
 Mastery of production pipelines; ability to lead diverse teams & manage schedules, documentation, QA, and more.
Detailed Curriculum (Click to Expand)

This course focuses on the organizational and leadership aspects of game dev. Students learn to break down features into Agile backlogs, estimate tasks, schedule sprints, and adapt to changes via iterative planning. Tools like Trello/Jira, Git/Perforce are introduced. A heavy emphasis is placed on team communication, conflict resolution, and building a healthy creative environment.

Relevance: The keystone for a Technical Game Producer is orchestrating design, art, and tech to meet deadlines. This course provides the frameworks (Scrum, Kanban) and best practices to keep projects stable, efficient, and fun to work on.

Resources & Examples: “Agile Game Development with Scrum” (Clinton Keith), GDC “Producer Bootcamp” talks, real AAA/indie postmortems on production successes and failures.

Capstone Project I: Multiplayer Pre-Production 2
  • Thesis concept pitch & design doc
  • Multiplayer architecture planning
  • Vertical slice or core prototype
  • Define project scope & schedule
  • Create GDD & netcode plan
  • Build vertical slice with partial features
  • Mid-semester pitch & milestone review
 A solid foundation for the Year 2 “thesis” game; validated core gameplay and feasible tech approach.
Detailed Curriculum (Click to Expand)

The fall portion of the Capstone focuses on pre-production for an ambitious (but scoped) multiplayer project. Students (often solo or with minimal help) propose a concept, draft a Game Design Document, and implement a vertical slice. Emphasis on networking feasibility, project scoping, and risk mitigation.

Relevance: Pre-production is where technical and creative plans converge. Producers who can validate design ideas early save huge resources later. By experiencing real “thesis” scoping, the student learns to combine design, programming, and production effectively.

Resources & Examples: - GDC “Vertical Slice” presentations
- Online playtest communities (e.g. r/playmygame) to gather user feedback
- Real project milestone templates (e.g. from FIEA or USC capstone outlines)

Detailed Curriculum (Click to Expand)

This course covers final publishing steps (store listings, marketing, platform requirements) and LiveOps (how to maintain and update a game post-release). Students learn about analytics, event scheduling, community engagement, and technical operations for a live multiplayer title.

Relevance: Understanding distribution channels, marketing funnels, and server management is vital for a Technical Game Producer. Modern games often run as live services; producers who can coordinate updates, handle data-driven decisions, and keep servers stable are highly valued.

Resources & Examples: - GDC talks on LiveOps best practices
- Steamworks or console certification docs
- AWS/Azure tutorials on hosting game servers & scaling

Capstone Project II: Production & Launch 4
  • Full production of the multiplayer “thesis”
  • Alpha/beta testing, polish
  • Public release or open beta
  • Live updates & postmortem
  • Implement remaining features & content
  • Conduct large-scale beta test
  • Release the game publicly & manage first updates
  • Write postmortem & complete final portfolio
 A fully launched multiplayer game; real-world experience with final deliverables, live patching, & project retrospective.
Detailed Curriculum (Click to Expand)

The second part of the Capstone sees full production and release of the student’s multiplayer game. Midway through the semester, they reach a playable beta (feature-complete) and invite external testers. They then finalize content, fix bugs, and orchestrate a public launch or open beta. Finally, they do a post-launch LiveOps event (like adding new content), gather data, and write a postmortem.

Relevance: This is the ultimate test of a Technical Producer’s skillset: leading a project through alpha, beta, launch, and live updates. Graduates can demonstrate real experience shipping a networked game, from core tech to marketing, QA, and beyond.

Resources & Examples: - Valve/Steam tutorials for shipping PC builds
- GDC “Launch Day Disasters” talks
- LiveOps frameworks for event-based content updates
