Self-Study Schedule Overview

This table summarizes an 8-part self-study path to become a Technical Game Producer. Each row outlines the module's focus, approximate timeline, major activities, and key outcome.

After each module row, expand the “Detailed Curriculum” section for a more in-depth breakdown.
Module Timeline Focus & Key Topics Activities Outcome
1. Games 101
(Game Literacy & Analysis)		 ~8 weeks
  • History of analog & digital games
  • Critical analysis & taxonomy of genres
  • Foundations: rules, systems, & player experience
  • Play classics (Chess, Go, retro video games)
  • Write short critical essays
  • Create a timeline of landmark games
  • Discuss cultural impact & design evolution
 Broad familiarity with game genres & history, plus the ability to analyze games from multiple angles.
Detailed Curriculum for Games 101 (Click to Expand)

Week-by-Week Schedule

Week Range Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Weeks 1-2
  • Foundations of Game Analysis
  • Basic Terminology (mechanics, dynamics, aesthetics)
  • Non-digital games & core systems
  • Play board/card games (Chess, Go, Poker)
  • Identify rules, objectives, strategies
  • Watch an intro lecture (e.g., MIT's “Intro to Game Design”)
  • Write a short reflection analyzing a board game's design
  • Rules of Play (Salen & Zimmerman)
  • MIT OpenCourseWare / edX
  • Chess.com for analysis
 Grasp of fundamental design vocabulary & ability to articulate basic game structure in non-digital examples.
Weeks 3-4
  • History of Video Games
  • Evolution of genres & platforms
  • Influential early arcade/console titles
  • Play/watch early classics (Space Invaders, Pac-Man)
  • Create a personal timeline of 10 landmark games
  • Short essay on tech's influence on design (2D to 3D)
  • The Ultimate History of Video Games (Steven Kent)
  • Documentaries (High Score, The King of Kong)
  • GDC Vault historical talks
 Understanding of historical context & how it shaped mechanics, genres, and industry trends.
Weeks 5-6
  • Critical Play & Genre Analysis
  • Formal elements vs. experiential elements
  • Playtesting with a critical lens
  • Play 2 games from genres outside your comfort zone
  • Analyze how mechanics & aesthetics create experience
  • Write a comparative analysis (e.g., sports vs. narrative)
  • Watch a GDC talk or read a postmortem on design decisions
  • Mary Flanagan's Critical Play
  • Extra Credits YouTube (game design series)
  • GDC Vault postmortems
 Systematic, analytical mindset for understanding design goals & trade-offs in various genres.
Weeks 7-8
  • Synthesis & Reflection
  • Cultural Context & Player Perspectives
  • Advanced Topics (ethics, representation)
  • Revisit a previously analyzed game for new insights
  • Explore cultural/ethical topics in a specific genre
  • Final reflective essay: “What Makes Games Meaningful?”
  • Present or discuss analyses in an online forum or with peers
  • Jesper Juul's Half-Real
  • A Theory of Fun (Raph Koster)
  • GameStudies.org for scholarly articles
 Deepened critical insight, examining games' social/cultural dimensions & overall significance.
2. Game Design I
(Fundamental Design Skills)		 ~8 weeks
  • Non-digital game creation
  • Iterative prototyping & playtesting
  • Design documentation & rule balancing
  • Paper prototypes of board/card games
  • Rapid iteration cycles
  • Blind playtesting & feedback loops
  • Simple GDD (Game Design Document)
 Iterative design skills, understanding of core mechanics & player-centric development approach.
Detailed Curriculum for Game Design I (Click to Expand)

Week-by-Week Schedule

Week Range Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Weeks 1-2
  • Design Foundations & System Thinking
  • Prototyping with analog tools
  • Core concepts of rules & play
  • Modify a simple game (Tic-Tac-Toe), observe changes
  • Study formal elements (players, objectives, procedures)
  • Watch a basic game design tutorial (Coursera, YouTube)
  • The Art of Game Design by Jesse Schell
  • “Introduction to Game Design” (Coursera)
  • Ernest Adams, Fundamentals of Game Design
 Introduction to iterative design & how rule tweaks affect overall gameplay.
Weeks 3-4
  • Paper Prototyping & Iteration Cycle
  • Hands-on creation of a board/card game
  • Playtesting & feedback
  • Create a simple board/card prototype from scratch
  • Conduct at least 2 major iterations based on test feedback
  • Document rule changes & rationale
  • Challenges for Game Designers, Brenda Romero & Ian Schreiber
  • Card stock, tokens, dice for physical prototyping
 Practice in rapid prototyping & refining designs through iterative playtests.
3. Game Studio I
(Intro to Digital Dev)		 ~9 weeks
(3 mini-projects)
  • Basic Unity projects
  • Collaboration & version control
  • Rapid prototyping & small game completions
  • Project 1: Solo (e.g., Pong clone)
  • Project 2: Small team game
  • Project 3: Join a Game Jam or short sprint
  • Use Git/Trello for planning & integration
 Understanding the digital pipeline and experience finishing multiple small playable prototypes.
Detailed Curriculum for Game Studio I (Click to Expand)

Approx. 9-Week Breakdown

Phase / Week Range Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Project 1
(Weeks 1-3)
  • Solo Development
  • Unity Basics (C# Scripting, Scenes, Collisions)
  • First Playable Prototype
  • Build a simple game (Pong, 2D shooter) following official Unity tutorials
  • Implement core gameplay & add 1 custom feature
  • Write a brief postmortem
  • Unity Learn
  • Beginner YouTube channels (Brackeys, etc.)
  • Git for version control (solo practice)
 Master basic Unity workflow, build confidence in coding & produce a playable mini-game alone.
Project 2
(Weeks 4-6)
  • Small Team Collaboration
  • Task Management (Trello, Git branches)
  • Enhanced Gameplay Features
  • Form a team (2-4 members) or simulate roles
  • Plan a slightly bigger concept (e.g., platformer with multiple levels)
  • Integrate frequent builds, maintain scope, hold team check-ins
  • Final team postmortem & playable release
  • Trello
  • Git / GitHub or other repo hosting
  • Optional: Discord/Slack for team comms
 Experience in collaborative dev, feature scoping, merging code/art & producing a more complete game.
Project 3
(Weeks 7-9)
  • Game Jam Simulation or Actual Jam
  • Intense time constraint
  • Finish & Polish quickly
  • Join a real jam (e.g., Global Game Jam, Ludum Dare) or do a 1-week sprint
  • Brainstorm around a theme, produce MVP in ~3 days-1 week
  • Focus on scope-limited, polished gameplay & 1-2 core features
  • Global Game Jam, Ludum Dare
  • Unity jam templates, quick prototypes
 Rapid dev under pressure; final jam game for portfolio & strong lessons in scoping & collaboration.
4. Code Lab 0
(Intro to Game Programming)		 ~8 weeks
  • Fundamental scripting concepts
  • GameMaker (GML) then Unity (C#)
  • Loops, variables, collisions, basic AI
  • GameMaker tutorials & small prototypes
  • Unity C# scripting (Roll-a-ball, etc.)
  • Debugging & engine familiarity
 Technical literacy to communicate with programmers, estimate tasks, and handle simple coding/bugfixes.
Detailed Curriculum for Code Lab 0 (Click to Expand)

Week-by-Week Outline

Week Range Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Weeks 1-2
  • GameMaker Basics (GML)
  • Variables, conditionals, collisions
  • First playable 2D prototype
  • Follow official GameMaker tutorial (e.g., a shooter/platformer)
  • Add custom behavior (e.g., extra enemy type)
  • Write code comments to clarify logic
  • GameMaker official site
  • GML scripting doc
  • Basic knowledge of 2D collisions
 Familiarity with an accessible 2D engine, basic coding constructs, & debugging.
Weeks 3-4
  • Advanced GML features / small expansions
  • Arrays, loops, data structures
  • Transition or compare with Unity approach
  • Implement more complex logic (e.g., spawn manager, AI patrol)
  • Study data structures (lists, arrays) for storing entities
  • Reflect on differences: GML vs. Unity C#
  • Official GML docs / tutorials
  • Community resources (YouTube, forums)
 Deeper coding confidence in 2D environment & awareness of cross-engine concepts.
Weeks 5-6
  • Unity & C# Scripting Intro
  • Basic 2D or 3D movement, collisions, prefab usage
  • OOP concepts (classes, components)
  • Complete a beginner Unity project (Roll-a-ball, Ruby's Adventure)
  • Learn collision triggers, transform updates
  • Use Debug.Log / console for debugging
  • Unity Learn
  • Visual Studio/VS Code with C#
  • Basic knowledge of OOP principles
 Understanding of component-based architecture, C# scripting, & Unity editor basics.
Weeks 7-8
  • Strengthening & Expanding Skills
  • Basic AI, data structures in C#
  • Small personal mini-project
  • Create a new small game or remake your GML project in Unity
  • Add simple AI (enemy chase, random movement)
  • Document your code & maintain a Git repo
  • Unity docs for advanced features
  • Git/GitHub for version control
  • Optional: advanced tutorials (Raycast, NavMesh, etc.)
 Solidify coding fundamentals, produce a standalone mini-project showcasing your new skills.
5. Visual Lab 0
(Fundamentals of Visual Design)		 ~8 weeks
  • Color theory, composition, typography
  • 2D asset creation & UI/UX basics
  • Digital art tools usage
  • Design simple sprites, logos, HUD elements
  • Learn Photoshop/GIMP basics
  • Wireframe and implement basic UI
  • Create short art style guide
 Foundational art literacy, ability to produce placeholders, and communicate effectively with artists.
Detailed Curriculum for Visual Lab 0 (Click to Expand)

Week-by-Week Outline

Week Range Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Weeks 1-2
  • Basic Drawing & Composition
  • Intro to Digital Tools (Photoshop/GIMP)
  • Line, shape, light/shadow
  • Doodle simple 2D objects (crates, potions) with shading
  • Explore layers, brushes, selection tools
  • Learn about rule of thirds & composition basics
  • GIMP.org
  • Photoshop tutorials
  • Basic drawing videos on YouTube
 Comfort with digital art tool UI, fundamental drawing skills & composition awareness.
Weeks 3-4
  • Color Theory & 2D Asset Creation
  • Sprites, tiles, or simple character art
  • Palette selection, shading, style consistency
  • Create a small sprite sheet (e.g., walk cycle or environment tiles)
  • Experiment with color palettes for mood
  • Study at least 1 game's art style & replicate a basic element
  • Color Theory references (Adobe Color, coolors.co)
  • Pixel art tutorials (e.g., Aseprite)
 Basic proficiency in creating 2D game art and color usage for visual clarity & style.
Weeks 5-6
  • UI/UX Design Principles
  • Wireframing HUDs & Menus
  • Typography & readability
  • Design a mock HUD for an existing or hypothetical game
  • Try a tool like Figma for UI layout
  • Focus on font pairing, icon design, color contrast
  • Figma or Adobe XD for UI mockups
  • Typography basics (serif vs. sans-serif, hierarchy)
 Ability to plan & prototype user interfaces that are clear & consistent.
Weeks 7-8
  • Art Style Guide & Integration
  • Polishing assets in-engine
  • Final small project or portfolio piece
  • Create a short style guide (color palette, fonts, references)
  • Import your assets into Unity or another engine
  • Adjust sprite settings, UI scaling, test performance
  • Unity's UI system docs
  • Optional: Blender or MagicaVoxel if curious about 3D
 Understand how to finalize & integrate 2D art assets in-engine, plus articulate a cohesive art style.
6. Game Studio II
(Capstone Team Project)		 ~10+ weeks
  • Larger scope & deeper collaboration
  • Full production pipeline (Alpha → Beta → Release)
  • Project management & team leadership
  • Form a bigger team or group
  • Develop a polished game over full cycle
  • Continuous integration, QA, final showcase
 Realistic production experience, culminating in a substantial portfolio-ready game.
Detailed Curriculum for Game Studio II (Click to Expand)

Phase-by-Phase Breakdown (~10 weeks)

Phase / Weeks Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Phase 1
(Weeks 1-2)
  • Concept & Planning
  • Scope, MVP, & risk analysis
  • Project management setup (Git, Trello/Jira)
  • Brainstorm feasible game ideas, pick one
  • Define core features & timeline (Alpha, Beta, Release)
  • Set up version control, decide roles (producer, coder, artist...)
  • Project management articles (e.g., agile, scrum) or GDC talks
  • Mentors/peers for feedback on scope
 Clear project vision & roadmap, well-defined scope to start development effectively as a team.
Phase 2
(Weeks 3-5)
  • Prototyping & Vertical Slice
  • Core mechanics, iterative testing
  • Early art/audio placeholders
  • Implement key gameplay loops quickly
  • Weekly playtests, gather feedback from team/outsiders
  • Adapt or cut features that don't work
  • Unity/Unreal engine docs
  • Continuous integration tools (GitHub Actions, etc.)
 A functional prototype demonstrating core gameplay & feasibility, guiding further content creation.
Phase 3
(Weeks 6-8)
  • Full Production
  • Content creation (levels, art, audio)
  • Team coordination, merges, regular QA
  • Produce final assets, build out levels
  • Integrate art & audio, maintain performance
  • Weekly sprints, track tasks in Trello/Jira
  • Midpoint external playtest for feedback
  • Team communication (Discord, Slack)
  • Agile sprint planning tutorials
 A near-complete, content-rich build ready for polish & final adjustments.
Phase 4
(Weeks 9-10)
  • Polish, QA, & Release
  • Bug-fixing & performance tuning
  • Final presentation or showcase
  • Thorough QA pass, fix critical issues
  • Polish visuals, audio mixing, add final touches
  • Prepare launch build, record trailer, do final postmortem
  • QA test plans or checklists
  • Video capture/edit tools (OBS, etc.)
 A fully playable, polished game for portfolio demonstration, reflecting real production experience.
7. Game Studies I
(Theory & Culture)		 ~6-8 weeks
(in parallel)
  • Scholarly research on games
  • Ludology vs. narratology
  • Player psychology, social/cultural impact
  • Read academic texts, Rules of Play, Jesper Juul
  • Write short analytical essays
  • Explore ethics, representation, monetization
 Deeper intellectual framework for understanding player motivations, cultural contexts, & industry issues.
Detailed Curriculum for Game Studies I (Click to Expand)

Week-by-Week Outline (~6-8 Weeks)

Week Range Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Weeks 1-2
  • Defining “Game Studies”
  • Ludology vs. Narratology
  • Player Types (Bartle, Yee)
  • Read Bartle's “Players Who Suit MUDs”
  • Analyze 1 game's appeal to each player type
  • Write short reflection: ludology vs. narratology differences
  • GameStudies.org papers
  • Nick Yee's Quantic Foundry blog
  • Jesper Juul's Half-Real
 Understanding basic academic debates & frameworks for categorizing players.
Weeks 3-4
  • Game Psychology & Engagement
  • Flow, immersion, motivation
  • Case studies (MMOs, mobile, etc.)
  • Explore research on flow theory (Csikszentmihalyi) in games
  • Pick a popular MMO or mobile game, dissect engagement loops
  • Write a short essay on how reward systems shape player behavior
  • A Theory of Fun (Raph Koster)
  • Selected GDC talks on player retention
 Ability to connect psychological theories with practical design elements that keep players engaged.
Weeks 5-6
  • Socio-Cultural Impact of Games
  • Diversity & representation
  • Ethical considerations (loot boxes, monetization)
  • Study controversies around loot boxes & gambling laws
  • Examine representation in a AAA or indie title
  • Participate in an online debate or forum discussion
  • IGDA resources on diversity
  • Industry articles on monetization & regulation
 Deeper insight into games' social responsibility & reflection on ethical design choices.
Weeks 7-8 (Optional)
  • Independent Research Project
  • Academic Writing & Citations
  • Presentation of findings
  • Pick a specialized topic (e.g., serious games, VR, or eSports culture)
  • Write a short research paper with references
  • Present or summarize in a blog post or peer group
  • Journals like Game Studies, Eludamos
  • Library/Google Scholar for academic papers
 Practical experience in academic research, honing writing & critical thinking about a chosen game-related topic.
8. Survival Skills
(Career & Industry Prep)		 ~4 weeks
(concurrent)
  • Résumé/portfolio building
  • Networking & job search strategies
  • Mock interviews, industry insights
  • Craft a targeted résumé & LinkedIn profile
  • Build an online portfolio (website, itch.io)
  • Attend/watch GDC talks
  • Practice interviews & apply to entry roles
 Polished professional presence, clear career goals, plan to enter the industry.
Detailed Curriculum for Survival Skills (Click to Expand)

Week-by-Week Outline (~4 weeks)

Week Range Focus & Key Topics Activities Resources Outcome
Week 1
  • Résumé & LinkedIn Setup
  • Portfolio Outline
  • Targeting Producer Roles
  • Create or refine a 1-page résumé highlighting team projects
  • Update LinkedIn with project details & a succinct summary
  • Plan your portfolio structure (sections for projects, about me)
  • Resume critique sites/tools (Cultivated Culture, etc.)
  • LinkedIn job search & “Open to Work” settings
 A professional brand that showcases your production & technical skills in a concise, attractive way.
Week 2
  • Online Portfolio & Repositories
  • Showcasing Gameplay & Collaboration
  • Build or update a personal site (Wix, GitHub Pages, Squarespace)
  • Upload playable prototypes to itch.io or share code on GitHub
  • Record short gameplay clips, add them to your site
  • Free website builders, HTML/CSS templates
  • Screen recording software (OBS, etc.)
 Employers can easily see your work, confirm your role, & understand your game dev process.
Week 3
  • Networking & Industry Research
  • Informational Interviews, IGDA/GDC communities
  • Join dev communities (IGDA local chapter, Discord servers)
  • Reach out for informational interviews with producers
  • Watch GDC Vault talks on production & career tips
  • IGDA, GDC Vault
  • LinkedIn “connections” approach
 Growing professional network, deeper insight into real producer roles, possible job leads or mentors.
Week 4
  • Mock Interviews & Applications
  • Planning Next Steps
  • Practice with common producer interview Q&A (team conflict, scope mgmt.)
  • Apply to at least 2-3 relevant junior/assoc. producer roles or internships
  • Map out a Plan B (QA or dev roles) if direct producer jobs are scarce
  • Glassdoor/Indeed for sample questions
  • Friends or mentors for mock interviews
 Confidence and readiness to tackle real interviews, with a fallback strategy for breaking into the industry.
